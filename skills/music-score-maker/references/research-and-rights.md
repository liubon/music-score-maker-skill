# Research, provenance, and rights

## Source order

Prefer evidence in this order:

1. User-provided audio, notation, or authorized source
2. Composer, artist, label, publisher, or official release page
3. Licensed sheet-music catalog metadata or a lawful score the user can access
4. Reputable music databases and scholarly catalogs
5. Multiple independent chord/analysis references
6. Aural inference from a lawfully accessible recording

Search snippets, crowd transcriptions, and automatically detected key/BPM values are leads, not ground truth.

## What to record

Keep the exact work title, artist/composer, version, release identifier or URL, evidence for key/meter/tempo, access date when useful, and which musical details were inferred. Cite direct pages rather than search-result pages.

## Rights guardrails

- Never seek pirated scans, unauthorized score dumps, leaked stems, DRM circumvention, or paywall bypasses.
- Do not copy a commercial engraving's layout, editorial markings, or proprietary fingering merely to recreate the product.
- It is safe to work from public-domain music, user-authored music, user-provided material they are entitled to use, and properly licensed sources.
- For a commercially released work, create a fresh transcription or arrangement from lawful evidence and label it accurately. Do not claim endorsement or official status.
- If lawful evidence is too limited for a reliable full transcription, produce a clearly labeled approximation, lead sheet, harmonic outline, or short sample and explain the limitation.
- Lyrics are separately protected in many jurisdictions. Include full lyrics only when user-provided, public domain, licensed, or otherwise clearly permitted; otherwise use limited alignment cues or omit them.

These are workflow safeguards, not legal advice. When publication, sale, public performance, or distribution is planned, advise the user to confirm the relevant composition, arrangement, lyric, mechanical, synchronization, and print rights.

## Confidence labels

- `high`: directly supported by a reliable source or clearly audible and cross-checked
- `medium`: musically coherent and supported indirectly, with minor uncertain details
- `low`: reconstructed from sparse, conflicting, or noisy evidence

Rate identity, harmony, and melody separately. Point to exact uncertain measures when possible.
